It's no secret that people seek instant graitification now more than ever. [76% of American's live pay check to pay check](https://money.cnn.com/2013/06/24/pf/emergency-savings/) and [47% of people expect a website to load in under 2 seconds](https://neilpatel.com/blog/loading-time/) to name two statistics. Technology is partly to blame for this rise. But this isn't an "old man yells at cloud" article (well sort of), it's an article about how instant gratification has affected software learning and delivery. How?

## Focus on 'Low-hanging' fruit rather then impactful change
If you've ever worked as a software developer, doubtless you will have heard the phrase "low-hanging" fruit. It conjures up the idea of a mango you can just snap from the shortest branches rather than taking the time to scramble up the branches to the peak. There are definitely times deploy low hanging fruit tickets. For example, when new members join your team/company, "low-hanging" fruit can be a great way to get the team member contributing code quickly.

But, I've observed (notice: "I've" so take it with a pinch of salt) that there is an almost cult-like obsession with boiling down tasks into a minim. Sometimes it is prudent to do so. First-principles thinking can clarify thought and help solve the *actual* problem rather than an imaginary one. Nonetheless, often it reaches a stage where nothing meaningful is being done by one single work item. Consequently, team members feel their impact is reduced, things ship slower and people tread on each others toes.
